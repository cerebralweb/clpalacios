The digital consumer: testing an app for a Health Care brand

César Luhmann

In this article ❧ We tell you how a healthcare company prototyped and tested a new product (and its app) combining online communities and face to face research. Here we have a case where the digital consumer is looming as a new category that could be remarkably important for marketing researchers (Read the footnote about the quoted sources)

You should be prepared to see things that you never expected to happen. Think about me, for example. I am a software engineer: and here I am, writing a article about consumption and marketing. Nowadays, nothing is strange, a marketer learning how to do software, a programmer learning how to market goods. Would it be strange to judge how much success a health product can have by analysing its digital context? No. Let's discuss this case to illustrate my point. (Please, read the footnote about the source)

A multinational healthcare brand

A large pharmaceutical business was beginning work developing an exciting new healthcare product which consisted of a device and an accompanying app. As the development (and the thinking for it) would evolve over a number of months, they were keen to set up an on-tap resource so they could validate their ideas and get feedback on designs as and when they were created. The client was working with a digital agency to bring the ideas to life and create wireframes and InVision prototypes for consumer feedback. The agency worked by means of two-week digital design sprints, which were modular in nature - building these up to create the broader app experience.

So, They needed an agile research project to keep pace with the sprint-plan:

  • The feedback could help the product and design team address any issues
  • Insights could inform thinking for subsequent sprints
We set up an online community with the target audience to deliver these insights and sync with the design sprints. The sample size (n=80) was large enough to give confidence in the findings, and we achieved strong retention rates -which was crucial as we needed to ensure we had a group of participants familiar with the product/app idea and each component part.

In this part, I only want to add that is curious how large this online community was. Comparing with online communities in Latin America (which used to recruit an average of 35 participants), makes me think that here money and economical context is playing a key role, or, in other words, that reasearchers in developed countries manage to handle more budget; but it is just an idea (another possible explanation could be related with less interest in data validity in the Latin American context).

However, there were some limitations to a purely online approach. Let's see:

  • There was limited scope to retest 'fixes' made to the modules (would have been too repetitive / boring for the community)
  • As modules were tested one at a time, it was difficult to be entirely sure how well they would link together within the whole experience. Would users understand the holistic experience? The online community had highlighted there may be some concerns in this respect.

So, the authors decided to use a hybrid design by combining the online community with analog researching:

We recommended F2F (Face to Face) UX (User Experience) sessions to:
  • Give us an opportunity to retest some of the 'fixes' made. Had the changes to the designs gone far enough to address the key issues flagged up in the online community?
  • Test the outputs of the sprints in a holistic way - test the whole experience (not module by module). Do users understand the end-to-end experience and how well does it all sync-up?
  • Confirm whether any aspects of the Ul would be overlooked when participating in user journeys.
We also thought that a F2F approach would be more powerful in giving the product team the chance to eyeball users and see any user difficulties first-hand vs.reading about it in a report.

The Outcome

The Face to Face sessions proved to be effective:

  • Confirmed that the majority of the UX worked well
  • Confirmed hypotheses that users would experience some confusion at certain junctures in the overall journey
  • Demonstrated the importance of addressing these issues to the client and design partner
  • Identified a few other issues with the company which would have been difficult to spot with a purely online approach.

There were some benefits from this hybrid approach: while the online research was agile and applied anywhere, the offline research was human; on the other hand, both were accurate. Finally, but not least: When recruiting for communities, it is often advisable to consider whether it worth boosting recruitment in close proximity to your offices - making it easier to ask participants to take part in F2F research if and when the need arises.
